Official Selection

1939 / JOHN FORD

Stagecoach

A classic Western following a group of strangers travelling through dangerous Apache territory. In this film, John Ford succeeded in establishing many of the visual and narrative conventions that would come to define the classical Western: iconic cinematic landscape, the moral opposition between civilisation and wilderness, and the centrality of rugged individualism embodied by the outlaw-cowboy, the Ringo Kid.

2013 / JIA ZHANGKE

A Touch of Sin

Inspired by real-life incidents, Jia, the award-winning six generation director, weaves together four loosely connected stories set in different regions of contemporary China. This film re-territorialises the Western’s violence into the lawless industrial hinterlands of contemporary China. By weaving the “canxia” (damaged knights-errant) with the figure of “outlaw-cowboy”, Jia demonstrates a transnational genre migration. It is a “Wuxia-Western” that deconstructs the myth of progress, proving that the frontier’s moral crisis is a shared global condition.

2005 / ANG LEE

Brokeback Mountain

This film follows the decades-spanning relationship between two cowboys, Ennis Del Mar and Jack Twist, whose emotional and sexual bond unfolds in the rugged landscapes of the American West. As a Taiwanese-born director working in Hollywood, Ang Lee brings an “outsider’s” sensibility to a typical American genre, reframing the Western through a more intimate, melancholic, and restrained aesthetic.

2017 / CHLOÉ ZHAO

The Rider

As a fellow alumnus of NYU Tisch School of the Arts (same university Ang Lee attended), Chloé Zhao epitomises accented cinema, as a Beijing-born filmmaker exploring the heart of the American Lakota community. Blurring the line between fiction and documentary, Zhao’s early work evokes the poetic realism of Terrence Malick, offering a meditative portrait of masculinity, resilience, and the tension between surrender and perseverance within the fading mythos of the American frontier.

2020 / CHLOÉ ZHAO

Nomadland

With this film, Zhao became the sixth Chinese filmmaker to win the Golden Lion at the Venice Film Festival, following Jia Zhangke (2006) and Ang Lee (2005, 2007). Continuing her signature blend of poetic realism and documentary aesthetics, Zhao reflects on precarity, mobility, and the lived realities of individuals navigating socio-economic decline, marking a profound reimagining of the frontier myth in contemporary America.

Curatorial Rationale

Investigating the migration of generic tropes across national boundaries.

NTFF 2026 investigates the transnational turn within cinema, specifically focusing on the migration and evolution of the Western. By tracing the arc from John Ford’s nation-building myths to the “accented cinema” of Chloé Zhao and Ang Lee, we deconstruct the Western myth.

Classical Western: Represented by “Stagecoach”, set in the 19th-century, it established the foundational mythos by linking territorial expansion with moral clarity.

Revisionist Western: Such as “Brokeback Mountain”, which deconstructs the traditional ideological core of the genre, including heteronormative masculinity, while maintaining a dialogue with its historical dimensions.

Post-Western: Including “A Touch of Sin”, “The Rider”, and “Nomadland”. These films function as post-Westerns by transposing traditional motifs including loneliness, lawlessness, and the quest for dignity into a globalised, contemporary landscape where the Western myth is no longer a promise of expansion, but a site of survival.
